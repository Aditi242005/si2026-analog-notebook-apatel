# SI-2026 Analog IC Design
This repository documents my summer-internship project of Analog Integrated-Circuit Design under the mentorship of **Dr. Saroj Rout** , **Mr Prasant Kumar Swain** , **Dr. Santunu Sarangi** in Silicon University, conducted in May - June, 2026 . 

# **COURSE OUTCOME**

- Design of Analog-IC using electronic system.
- Deriving all the IC specifications using the circuit Analysis.
- A good understanding of MOS devices and Technology.
- Proficiency in analog circuit and design analysis.
- Having a good mastery in some tools--ngspice, xschem, magic.

## Daily Report
## Day 1
The first day of the training program provided an introduction to the course, highlighting its objectives, structure, and expected learning outcomes. We were introduced to the USB microphone system, where we learned about its architecture, operating principles, and the process of capturing and processing audio signals. The session also covered the fundamentals of Linux and Git, emphasizing the importance of Linux command-line usage and Git version control in engineering workflows. To evaluate our foundational understanding, a basic circuit quiz was conducted on topics such as Ohm's law, resistive circuits, and voltage-current relationships. Overall, the day established a strong foundation by combining hardware concepts, essential software tools, and theoretical knowledge, preparing us for the subsequent technical sessions.
## Day 2
The second day of training focused on the fundamentals of linear circuit analysis. We studied key concepts including Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL), Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL), Superposition Theorem, Node and Mesh Analysis, Thevenin’s Theorem, Norton’s Theorem, and the Maximum Power Transfer Theorem. These principles are essential for analyzing and simplifying electrical circuits. The practical session involved using Ngspice and Xschem to simulate circuits such as voltage dividers and MOSFET ID–VGS characteristics using the Level-1 model. The simulations helped reinforce the theoretical concepts by providing a clear understanding of circuit behavior and device characteristics. Overall, the day effectively combined analytical techniques with hands-on simulation experience, strengthening our foundation in circuit analysis and design.
## Day 3
The third day of training focused on the transient analysis of RC circuits and the fundamentals of operational amplifier (Op-Amp) circuits. We studied the charging and discharging behavior of capacitors, the concept of the RC time constant (τ), and its effect on transient responses. Various numerical problems were solved to strengthen our understanding of transient analysis and circuit behavior. The session also covered basic Op-Amp configurations, including inverting and non-inverting amplifiers, integrators, and differentiators, along with their practical applications in analog signal processing. During the laboratory session, we used Ngspice and Xschem to simulate RC circuits and observe their step and pulse responses under different RC values. These simulations reinforced the theoretical concepts by demonstrating how circuit parameters influence waveform characteristics. Overall, the day provided a solid understanding of transient circuit analysis and the practical applications of Op-Amp-based analog circuits.
## Day 4
The fourth day of training focused on the frequency response of linear circuits, with an emphasis on high-pass and low-pass filters. We learned how these filters selectively allow or attenuate signals based on their frequency and explored their significance in signal processing, communication systems, and electronic circuit design. The theoretical session included the analysis of both passive RC filters and active filter circuits, providing insight into their operating principles and frequency characteristics. During the laboratory session, we were introduced to the ADALM2000, a multifunctional hardware instrument used for circuit testing and measurement. We learned how to configure the device, perform signal measurements, and analyze circuit responses experimentally. This hands-on experience reinforced the theoretical concepts by demonstrating the practical implementation and testing of frequency-dependent circuits. Overall, the day enhanced our understanding of filter design, frequency response, and the use of modern electronic measurement tools
## Day 5
The fifth day of training focused on modeling the MEMS microphone using its Thevenin equivalent circuit, demonstrating how complex systems can be represented by simpler equivalent models for easier analysis. We also studied the Analog Front-End (AFE) of the microphone system, understanding its role in signal amplification, conditioning, and preparation for further processing. During the laboratory session, we continued working with the ADALM2000 to perform experiments that validated the theoretical concepts discussed in class. These practical exercises provided insights into the interaction between the MEMS microphone and the amplifier while highlighting the importance of equivalent circuit models in predicting system behavior. Overall, the day strengthened our understanding of circuit modeling, analog front-end design, and the practical verification of theoretical concepts using modern electronic measurement equipment.
## Day 6
The sixth day of training was primarily focused on laboratory exercises and practical implementation. We began by modeling the MEMS microphone and its Analog Front-End (AFE) to understand how acoustic signals are converted into electrical signals and subsequently amplified and conditioned for further processing. The session included analyzing equivalent circuit models and simulating their behavior to gain deeper insight into the operation of the microphone system. We also continued working with the ADALM2000, learning how to configure the hardware, connect measurement probes, and perform signal analysis. Practical experiments on voltage divider circuits and Thevenin equivalent circuits further reinforced the theoretical concepts covered in previous sessions. Overall, the day provided valuable hands-on experience in circuit modeling, simulation, and experimental verification, strengthening our understanding of MEMS-based audio systems and analog circuit design.
## Day 7 
The seventh day of training introduced us to the Integrated Circuit (IC) design flow, providing an overview of the complete semiconductor design process. We learned about the various stages involved, including specification development, schematic design, circuit simulation, layout design, physical verification, and fabrication. The session emphasized the significance of each stage in ensuring the functionality, performance, and manufacturability of integrated circuits, while also highlighting the collaborative nature of IC development. During the laboratory session, we performed microphone and Analog Front-End (AFE) testing and characterization, where we measured key performance parameters such as gain, bandwidth, and noise characteristics. The experimental results were compared with theoretical and simulated values to evaluate design accuracy and identify any deviations. Overall, the day enhanced our understanding of the IC design workflow and underscored the importance of device characterization in validating and optimizing analog circuit performance.
## Day 8
The eighth day of training focused on passive electronic devices, including resistors, capacitors, and inductors, which serve as the fundamental building blocks of electronic circuits. We studied their construction, operating principles, electrical characteristics, and behavior under different operating conditions, with particular emphasis on their frequency-dependent response. The session also explored the applications of passive components in circuits such as filters, timing networks, voltage dividers, and impedance matching systems. In addition, we discussed how passive devices interact with active components to achieve the desired circuit performance in both analog and digital designs. Although no laboratory session was conducted, the theoretical discussions provided a strong foundation for understanding the role of passive components in practical circuit design. Overall, the day strengthened our knowledge of the fundamental elements that form the basis of more advanced electronic systems.
## Day 9
The ninth day of training focused on semiconductor IC devices, with particular emphasis on MOS (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) devices and their role in integrated circuit design. We began by studying the fundamentals of semiconductor physics, including the properties of silicon, PN junctions, diodes, and the operating principles of MOSFETs. The session covered key concepts such as threshold voltage, channel formation, device operation in different regions, and the impact of scaling on modern semiconductor technology. We also revisited the IC design flow, reinforcing the stages from specification and schematic design to simulation, layout, verification, and fabrication, providing a comprehensive understanding of the complete design process. In addition, we completed a SiliWiz assignment, where we applied theoretical concepts through device-level simulations to better understand semiconductor behavior and MOSFET characteristics. Overall, the day strengthened our understanding of semiconductor devices, their significance in analog and digital IC design, and the role of simulation tools in analyzing and validating device performance.
## Day 10
The tenth day of training focused on MOS transistor parameter extraction and device modeling through laboratory experiments. We learned how to extract important MOSFET parameters, including threshold voltage (VTH), transconductance (gm), and subthreshold characteristics, from experimental measurements. Using the ADALM2000 and Scopy, we performed electrical measurements on MOS transistor circuits and analyzed the collected data to understand device behavior under different operating conditions. The extracted parameters were then incorporated into SPICE models to simulate transistor performance accurately, highlighting the relationship between experimental results and circuit simulation. In addition, reference material based on Kang et al. provided deeper insights into MOSFET operation, device scaling, and modeling techniques used in integrated circuit design. Overall, the session enhanced our understanding of semiconductor device characterization, parameter extraction, and SPICE-based modeling, providing valuable practical experience in transistor-level analysis and simulation.
## Day 11
The eleventh day of training focused on the electrical characteristics and parameters of MOSFETs. We studied the operation of MOS transistors in the cutoff, triode, and saturation regions, along with the influence of key parameters such as threshold voltage (VTH), carrier mobility (μ), and oxide capacitance (Cox) on device performance. The session included an analysis of the current-voltage (I-V) characteristics, illustrating how the drain current varies with gate-to-source and drain-to-source voltages. These concepts provided a deeper understanding of MOSFET behavior in both analog and digital circuit applications. During the laboratory session, we used Xschem to experimentally measure MOSFET characteristics and compare the results with theoretical predictions. Overall, the day strengthened our understanding of MOSFET operation, parameter extraction, and the interpretation of I-V characteristics, providing a solid foundation for transistor-level circuit analysis and design.
## Day 12
The twelfth day of training focused on the design and analysis of current mirrors, which are fundamental building blocks in analog integrated circuits. We began by studying the simple current mirror, understanding how matched MOS transistors replicate a reference current to generate stable bias currents. The session then covered the cascode current mirror, highlighting its improved output resistance, reduced channel-length modulation effects, and enhanced current matching accuracy. Finally, we explored the wide-swing current mirror, which offers a larger output voltage swing while maintaining good current accuracy, making it suitable for low-voltage analog designs. Each current mirror topology was discussed in terms of its operating principle, advantages, limitations, and practical applications in biasing networks and analog amplifier circuits. During the laboratory session, we implemented and simulated these current mirror configurations using SPICE, analyzing their current transfer characteristics, output compliance, and overall performance. Overall, the day provided a comprehensive understanding of current mirror architectures and their significance in achieving accurate biasing and reliable analog circuit operation.
## Day 13
The thirteenth day of training focused on the fundamentals of differential amplifiers, one of the most important building blocks in analog integrated circuit design. We studied the operating principles of differential pairs, including differential and common-mode signals, biasing techniques, common-mode rejection, and voltage gain. The session also introduced the small-signal analysis of differential amplifiers, enabling us to understand key performance parameters such as differential gain, common-mode gain, input resistance, and output resistance. We discussed the significance of differential amplifiers in enhancing noise immunity and signal integrity, as well as their role as the input stage of operational amplifiers and other analog circuits. During the laboratory session, we simulated differential amplifier circuits using SPICE, investigating the effects of varying bias currents and transistor dimensions on circuit performance. Overall, the day provided a strong theoretical and practical foundation in differential amplifier design, preparing us for the study of more advanced analog circuit architectures
## Day 14
The fourteenth day of training focused on the fundamentals of Integrated Circuit (IC) layout design. We were introduced to the layout design process, including device placement, interconnect routing, and the importance of adhering to fabrication design rules. The session covered key concepts such as Design Rule Check (DRC) and Layout Versus Schematic (LVS) verification, emphasizing their role in ensuring that layouts are both manufacturable and electrically consistent with the original schematic. We also discussed layout techniques such as symmetry, device matching, and careful routing to minimize parasitic effects and improve the performance and reliability of analog circuits. During the laboratory session, we gained hands-on experience with IC layout tools by creating basic MOS transistor layouts and routing interconnections while following the required design rules. Overall, the day highlighted the critical role of layout in the IC design flow and demonstrated how careful physical design directly influences circuit performance, yield, and successful fabrication.
## Day 15
The fifteenth day of training focused on the design and stability analysis of two-stage amplifiers. We began by revisiting small-signal analysis and frequency response concepts before extending them to multi-stage amplifier architectures. The session introduced key stability parameters, including phase margin and gain margin, and explained their significance in determining the stability and transient performance of amplifiers. We also studied Miller compensation, a widely used frequency compensation technique that improves amplifier stability by introducing a dominant pole, while discussing the trade-offs between gain, bandwidth, and stability. During the laboratory session, we simulated two-stage amplifier circuits using SPICE, performed AC analysis to generate Bode plots, and identified the dominant poles and zeros affecting the frequency response. We further investigated the effect of varying the compensation capacitor on amplifier stability and bandwidth. Overall, the day provided a comprehensive understanding of two-stage amplifier design, frequency compensation techniques, and the importance of achieving an optimal balance between gain, bandwidth, and stable operation in analog integrated circuits.
